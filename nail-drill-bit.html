Types of Nail Drill Bits

There are a wide variety of nail drill bits to choose from, each with its unique applications and functions.

Carbide bits

Carbide bits are made with cemented carbide metal and feature slits resembling grooves. As opposed to scraping as a diamond drill bit would, a carbide drill bit scrapes away the nail finish thanks to these slits. Experienced users will benefit most from using these because they’re effective in breaking up acrylic resin. 

Mandrel bits 

Mandrel bits are constructed of rubber or metal.  You can just fit the mandrel top into the sanding band and you're good to go. Remember, you can’t sterilize the sanding band, which is one reason sanding bands are made of disposable paper bits.

Ceramic-coated bits

Ceramic drills don't heat like regular drills, which is an advantage of ceramic-coated bits. Additionally, they feature flute-shaped cuts that assist the drill bit in removing the product.

Diamond drill bits

The vast majority of nail drill bits are made using diamonds. Diamond drill bits can be derived from either natural or synthetic sources and are the toughest drill bits available. They’re used to scrape accumulated materials, but in contrast to the drill bits stated above, produce more friction and dust, resulting in higher heat.   

Different Nail Drill Bit Shapes

Sanding bands and mandrel bits

The mandrel bit is useful with sanding bands of varying grits. Depending on the intended application, they’re available in fine, medium, and coarse textures. Sanding bands are great for eliminating gloss from the nail before applying a product, and they’re simple enough for beginners to use. 

Barrel bits

Barrels remove or shape various nail treatments, including gels, poly gels, acrylic, and dips. This flat-topped form is great for trimming and shaping fake nails to the desired length. Also, the top edge can be useful to tap out lifted substances or to form grin lines.

Needle bits

Due to their extremely pointed and slender needle-like design, these are ideal for treatments in tight spaces, like under the nail. They’re also ideally suited for creating nail art designs with a gap, as they can be precisely targeted.

Cone bits

Conical in shape, these bits are ideal for removing acrylic or gel nail polish. Their distinctive shape complements your natural nail curvature beautifully.

Ball bits

This kind of nail drill bit is ideal for cuticle cleaning. After removing the cuticle from the nail plate, outline the nail's edge with the bit. Depending on the kind of skin, the bit can serve as a substitute for cuticle cutters. Also, it's excellent for removing and cleaning tough skin, as well as cutting edges for a more refined appearance.

Flame bits

After initial treatments, the flame bit is good for cleaning up all around the nail plate and enhancing the cuticle. Due to its compact size, it’s also effective for corners, sidewalls, and the removal of hangnails.

Safety bits

Generally, safety bits have a rounded top that is very smooth. They let you confidently cut near the cuticle and skin when filing in this delicate area. Due to the enhanced safety, you can practice without easily harming anyone, making them ideal for novices.

How To Maintain and Store Drill Bits

Correct cleaning and storage procedures increase the longevity of nail drill bits and can also prevent the transmission of illnesses from one client to another. Here are a few of the most effective and simple cleaning tips.

Cleaning

After each use, soak the bits in a warm, soapy water dish and scrub your equipment with a brushing bit until they are free of dirt and debris.

Disinfecting

You can place your drill bits in a fluid disinfectant or UV light sanitizer container. UV-C light is proven to kill 99.9% of dangerous bacteria, allowing you to eliminate them on nail drill bits and artificial nail pieces.

Storage

Put your bits in a holder so they stay neatly organized and may be displayed attractively.
